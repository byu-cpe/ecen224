## TL;DR

- **ECEn 225 (Coding):** No AI use of any kind, period. First offense gets a warning. Second offense gets a zero on the assignment–no exceptions.

- **ECEn 224 (Problem Solving):** AI is only allowed during instructor-led, in-classroom study sessions. All other uses of AI are prohibited unless the instructor explicitly permits them.


## Guiding Principles

These policies are grounded in the following educational goals:

1. **Reduce "effort outsourcing":** When AI is allowed to solve labs and problems for students, they are effectively outsourcing their efforts. An integral part of these introductory classes is building the necessary skills and knowledge to tackle real-world engineering problems, which requires students to invest personal time in learning, failing, and developing.

2. **Provide realistic expectations:** Engineering is a difficult discipline, and this class provides a reasonable but challenging entry point designed to communicate the need for dedication and effort. If students outsource challenges to AI early on, they put themselves at risk of being unable to complete the major when later challenges cannot be solved by AI. It is better to discover the major is not the right fit in the first class, not the fifth.

3. **Increase student engagement:** Provide motivation and reasoning for students to develop skills without the assistance of AI. Demonstrate the value of a personal knowledge base, and encourage students to participate and collaborate in their learning. Heightened student collaboration improves test scores, increases major completion rates, and helps prepare students to work on real engineering teams.

4. **Train students on the use of AI as a tool:** Help students gain perspective on their use of AI within the university. Demonstrate appropriate and contained use of artificial intelligence, and show the capacity of AI to assist in the *learning* process rather than the *answering* process.


## ECEn 225: Coding

In the context of ECEn 225, the use of AI in any capacity is not allowed. Students are expected to develop these basic coding skills *before* they learn how to use advanced tools, including AI, to assist them in the design process.

## ECEn 224: Problem Solving

The use of AI will be strictly limited to **in-classroom study events**, where instructors can show or suggest direct prompts students can use to ask AI questions on specific topics or create practice problems. Such review sessions allow the professor to work effectively with all students at once, while simultaneously demonstrating an understanding of AI's effectiveness without allowing it to hinder learning.

Without the express consent of the instructor, **all other use of AI is prohibited**.

## Enforcement Policy

Because this policy is part of the classroom standard, using AI where prohibited constitutes an unfair advantage and is considered cheating. This policy enforces a **one-warning system**:

1. **First offense:** Students using AI are given a clear, friendly, and direct reminder of the policy.
2. **Second offense:** Students who continue to use AI where prohibited will receive a **0 on that assignment**, without question.

AI may be able to solve these problems, but doing so is a form of cheating. Students should not receive credit for what is effectively someone else's work.
